The Problem

City government websites are organized around government, not citizens.

Boston.gov had content spread across 50 department sites—each with its own navigation, architecture, language. Where do I pay a parking ticket? Parking Services? Transportation? Treasury? What do I do when it snows? (There are literal fistfights over parking when it snows in Boston.) Does the department that handles tickets also prevent fistfights?

Citizens don't think in departments. They think in problems.

The question: How do you reorganize a city's entire web presence around how people actually need help?

The System

Working with IDEO, I helped design a system that cuts across departmental silos.

Strategic Principles

Research revealed the core insight: citizens don't care about org charts. They care about getting things done. The positioning became simple: act as a helpful human. Every design decision filtered through that lens.

Component Architecture

I designed a system of reusable components for the CMS—not page templates, but building blocks that could assemble into different configurations based on citizen need. Defined once, deployed everywhere.

Topic Pages

This was the key intervention. Instead of routing citizens through department navigation, topic pages organize content by problem—how a user thinks about it, not what department owns it.

"Winter in Boston" pulls from multiple departments: parking rules, snow removal, heating assistance, school closings. One page. One mental model. Fifty departments, invisible.

The system doesn't require departments to reorganize. It creates a citizen-facing layer on top of existing structure.

What This Enables

The old model: citizens navigate bureaucracy. Government publishes by department. Finding information requires knowing how government is organized.

The new model: government navigates itself. Citizens describe problems. The system assembles answers from wherever they live.

Pilot. Beta. Full launch. Positive outcomes from city and citizens alike.

The Difference

A conventional case study shows the homepage and the topic page templates.

This one is about the layer underneath: strategic principles that filter decisions, component architecture that enables flexibility, a content layer that makes fifty departments work as one.
