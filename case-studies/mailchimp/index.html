The Problem

Mailchimp's customers knew how to use Mailchimp. They didn't know how to do marketing.

The product had a detailed knowledgebase—buttons and dials, clicks and drags. But knowing how to create a campaign doesn't tell you why to create one. Or what to say. Or who to say it to. Or when.

Instructional content for beginners was out of date—often flat wrong. Valid content lived on a separate server with a fraction of the traffic. And help wasn't available where you needed it: in front of you, in the application, while working on a campaign.

The question: How do you teach people why to do marketing, not just how to use your tool?

The System

I founded and led a department producing educational content—not product documentation, but domain education. Marketing education.

The Distinction

Product knowledge lives in the knowledgebase: "Click here to add a merge tag."

Domain knowledge lives in educational content: "Why segmentation matters more than list size."

Mailchimp already had the first. I built the second.

The Infrastructure

We audited all existing content: Keep, Edit, or Kill. Surviving content was modeled for structure inside a CMS we built. I designed a customized WordPress installation with custom fields to match our content model.

The Stray Cat Program

You can't produce good educational content without a mechanism for the team to acquire real expertise. I instituted a program where team members could adopt "stray cat" topics—areas of marketing they'd teach themselves and then teach customers. The result: people who actually knew their subjects, not content writers faking expertise.

Cross-Channel Distribution

Educational content wasn't siloed on a blog. We built a publishing system to distribute content throughout the Mailchimp ecosystem—in-app, in onboarding, in email nurture sequences, on social. The same underlying content, surfaced where it mattered.

What This Enables

The old model: customers learn the product, figure out marketing on their own, churn when they can't translate tool knowledge into business results.

The new model: customers learn marketing through the product. The platform doesn't just enable campaigns—it teaches you why those campaigns matter.

A tool helps you execute. A system helps you understand what to execute and why.

The Difference

A conventional case study shows guides and blog posts and video content.

This one is about the system underneath: content model, expertise acquisition mechanism, distribution infrastructure. The machinery that turns domain knowledge into customer capability.

I didn't build a content library. I designed a system for manufacturing marketing competence.
